# Aesthetic System

Use this reference to derive a specific visual direction after choosing the information template. Do not copy a living artist or protected artwork. Translate references into abstract rules for composition, type, color, image treatment, texture, and rhythm.

## 1. Distill a reference into six dimensions

Separate visible evidence from interpretation.

| Dimension | Record |
| --- | --- |
| Composition | Grid, balance, focal position, overlap, crop, negative space |
| Typography | Family category, scale contrast, weight, line height, alignment, display treatment |
| Color | Main field, text colors, accent count, saturation, contrast allocation |
| Image | Subject role, crop, scale, lighting, background separation, collage behavior |
| Material | Paper, grain, glass, metal, fabric, ink, halftone, or no texture |
| Rhythm | Repetition, density changes, quiet zones, dividers, numbering, progression |

Output reusable rules, not a description of every visible object. Mark uncertain inferences as uncertain.

## 2. Set four design dials

Use values from 1 to 5.

- `COMPOSITION_VARIANCE`: 1 = strict symmetry; 5 = controlled collision and asymmetry.
- `INFORMATION_DENSITY`: 1 = one message; 5 = dense catalog or rules.
- `COMMERCIAL_INTENSITY`: 1 = editorial/brand; 5 = strong price and benefit signaling.
- `MATERIALITY`: 1 = flat/clean; 5 = pronounced physical texture and depth.

Do not maximize all four. Most strong posters have one or two dominant dials.

## 3. Choose or derive an aesthetic profile

### Brand Minimal

- Best for premium brands, hero products, invitations, and restrained announcements.
- Use large negative space, one focal product, refined type contrast, and one restrained accent.
- Keep commercial intensity at 1-2 unless a benefit is mandatory.
- Avoid fake gold gradients, excessive embossing, beauty-ad clichés, and tiny unreadable luxury type.

### Soft Editorial Lifestyle

- Best for food, beauty, home, travel, coffee, craft, and human-centered content.
- Use warm natural light, authored still-life crops, tactile but quiet materials, and magazine-like grouping.
- Pair a readable Chinese text face with one display category; keep color low to medium saturation.
- Avoid generic beige moodboards, random handwritten English, and decorative props with no subject role.

### Bold Typographic

- Best for announcements, campaign statements, youth culture, sports, music, and strong event drops.
- Let one headline or numeral become the graphic event; use extreme scale contrast and controlled edge tension.
- Protect the complete title, date, and benefit path even when secondary type crops.
- Avoid multiple competing headline treatments, rainbow accents, and uncontrolled type collisions.

### Festive Commerce

- Best for major promotions, benefit-heavy live events, countdowns, and red-envelope interactions.
- Use a strong accent field, clear benefit container, high product recognizability, and fast three-second parsing.
- Allocate contrast: title and benefit may both be strong, but not inside the same unresolved zone.
- Avoid filling every gap with coins, rays, confetti, badges, or 3D symbols.

### Clean Catalog

- Best for product lists, schedules, comparison cards, and multi-brand assortments.
- Use consistent card geometry, aligned baselines, normalized product images, and limited color coding.
- Create emphasis through scale and grouping rather than a different effect on every card.
- Avoid dashboard-like UI, excessive rounded cards, weak thumbnail-sized products, and mixed shadow directions.

### Cultural Material

- Best for regional food, crafts, heritage, travel, and thematic long-form stories.
- Derive patterns and materials from the actual subject; use them as frame, rhythm, or field rather than wallpaper.
- Preserve content hierarchy above atmosphere. Use captions, locations, and chapter markers as real metadata.
- Avoid generic Chinese-style clouds, seals, brush textures, and motifs unrelated to the subject.

## 4. Create a compact design philosophy

For generation or substantial redesign, write 3-5 internal sentences that define:

1. What should dominate at first glance.
2. How space and scale communicate the message.
3. How color allocates attention.
4. How image and material support the subject.
5. What must be deliberately absent.

Keep the philosophy specific enough to guide decisions but not so rigid that every output becomes the same template.

## 5. Anti-AI-slop rules

Reject these defaults unless the brief explicitly justifies them:

- purple-blue mesh gradient as automatic technology styling;
- centered title above floating glass cards;
- three equal cards used without an information reason;
- generic sparkles, stars, arrows, coins, blobs, or sticker clusters;
- fake interface screenshots, fake charts, and meaningless English labels;
- bevel, metallic, glow, outline, shadow, and extrusion stacked on the same title;
- every object floating with a separate soft shadow;
- decorative microtype that contains no real information;
- product photography reduced to background wallpaper;
- symmetry created by duplicating elements that should have different importance.

Use a **style budget**:

- one dominant graphic event;
- one primary title treatment;
- one main accent strategy;
- one material language;
- no decoration without a role.

## 6. Reference matching boundaries

- Preserve the user's designated roles for each reference.
- Match visual grammar and quality level, not protected characters, logos, or distinctive artwork unless supplied and authorized.
- If a reference conflicts with required facts, accessibility, or platform crop safety, preserve the facts and explain the adaptation.
- When the user says “keep everything else unchanged,” treat all unspecified content and geometry as invariants.
